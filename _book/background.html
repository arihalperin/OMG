4 Background

Rising atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO2) concentrations, caused primarily by land use changes and fossil fuel emissions, promote increases in CO2 concentrations within the ocean's surface waters. This increase in CO2 facilitates a process which causes a decrease in the pH of seawater. This process is known as ocean acidification. Changes in seawater pH shift the chemical equilibrium of three carbonate ions found in seawater. The ions affected are: carbon dioxide in solution (CO2(aq)) , also identified as carbonic acid (H2CO3), bicarbonate ion (HCO3^1-), and the carbonate ion (CO3^2-). Shifts in the concentrations of these ions affects the biological production of calcium carbonate (CaCO3) skeletons which many oceanic organisms build. Many organisms on coral reefs produce CaCO3 structures, in fact coral reefs themselves are made of CaCO3. Monitoring the shifting chemical environment in which these organisms live, and understanding the biogeochemical responses to ocean acidification at the reef are the primary goals of CRED and this understanding is critical for predicting and conserving reef ecosystem health. The measurement of dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) and total alkalinity (TA) concentrations in seawater resolve the carbonate ion system to which the ocean's coral reefs are tied. Having accurate values for these two parameters allows scientists to calculate pH and saturation state, with respect to calcite and aragonite (both are CaCO3 mineral structures built by calcifying organisms like corals, crustose coralline algae, and phytoplankton).

In order to calculate DIC and TA in a laboratory, measurements of salinity, pressure and temperature are also needed. Temperature and pressure measurements will either be collected with a CTD profiler or a handheld Subsurface Temperature and Pressure Recorder (STPR) which is a SBE39 with pressure sensor from SeaBird Electronics. Salinity samples will be collected from the same Niskin bottle from which the water for DIC/TA analysis is taken.

The water sample bottles and stoppers come from PMEL in a plastic tote with foam inserts. The bottles and stoppers have been soap washed, thoroughly rinsed, and muffled to 500 degrees C. Effort should be made to not get the bottles dirty or wet during transportation to the sample site, especially the bottle's interior. Contact with seawater or rain can contaminate the bottles, as well as, possibility fix the stopper inside the bottle's neck (as the bottle neck and stopper body are machined to a very close tolerance).
